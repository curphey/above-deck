## The Goal of Good Provisioning

Good provisioning is not about gourmet meals at sea. It is about making sure everyone on board is well-fed, hydrated, and energised throughout the passage — including when conditions are rough and no one feels like cooking.

The best passage meals are simple, nutritious, high in calories, and easy to prepare in a moving galley. Plan for the worst conditions and you will eat well in all of them.

## Planning Meals

Start with the basics: how many people, how many days, and how many meals per day. Then add a buffer — always provision for 50% longer than your expected passage time. A 10-day crossing should have 15 days of food on board. Weather delays, equipment problems, and calms happen.

### Structure Your Menu
Plan a rotating menu of 4-5 dinners, 2-3 lunch options, and a standard breakfast. This keeps variety without overcomplicating things.

**Breakfast ideas:**
- Porridge with dried fruit and honey (one-pot, filling, easy in rough weather)
- Granola with long-life milk
- Eggs (they last weeks unrefrigerated if unwashed — more on this below)
- Toast or wraps with peanut butter

**Lunch ideas:**
- Wraps with tinned tuna, cheese, and whatever vegetables are still fresh
- Tinned soup heated on the stove — fast and warm on a cold watch
- Couscous or pasta salad made in bulk, eaten cold over two days

**Dinner ideas:**
- One-pot curries with rice (tinned chickpeas, coconut milk, curry paste)
- Pasta with jarred sauce, tinned meat, and whatever veg is available
- Stews and chilli made with tinned beans and tomatoes
- Rice and beans with spices — cheap, filling, nutritious

### Pre-Cook Before Departure
The first day or two at sea is when seasickness is most likely. Cook 2-3 meals before you leave and store them ready to reheat. Stews, chilli, and curry all reheat well. Having food ready eliminates the need to cook when the crew is still finding their sea legs.

## What Lasts Without Refrigeration

Refrigeration on a boat is power-hungry and the fridge is usually small. Knowing what survives without it expands your options significantly.

**Weeks to months without refrigeration:**
- Root vegetables: potatoes, onions, carrots, sweet potatoes, beetroot
- Hard cabbage (lasts 3-4 weeks in a cool, ventilated spot)
- Citrus fruits: oranges, lemons, limes (2-3 weeks)
- Apples (2-3 weeks if individually wrapped in newspaper)
- Eggs (unwashed, unrefrigerated eggs last 3-4 weeks; washed eggs must be refrigerated)
- Hard cheeses sealed in wax (Gouda, Edam, Parmesan)
- Cured meats: salami, chorizo, jerky

**Pantry staples with long shelf life:**
- Rice, pasta, couscous, lentils, dried beans
- Tinned goods: tuna, chicken, chickpeas, beans, tomatoes, coconut milk, soups
- Peanut butter, honey, jam
- Crackers, tortilla wraps, long-life bread
- Dried fruit, nuts, chocolate, energy bars
- UHT (long-life) milk
- Coffee, tea, powdered drinks
- Cooking oil, soy sauce, hot sauce, spice jars

### Storage Tips
- **Stow heavy items low.** Tins in the bilge or deep lockers keep the centre of gravity down and free up accessible storage for lighter items.
- **Remove excess packaging.** Cardboard attracts moisture and cockroaches. Remove outer boxes and label tins with a permanent marker if the labels might come off.
- **Use net bags** for hanging fruit and vegetables. Air circulation prevents rot.
- **Rotate stock.** Put new provisions at the back and use older items first.
- **Vacuum seal** anything that can benefit from it — cheese, dried goods, snacks.

## Water Management

Water is more critical than food. You can go weeks without eating if you must. You cannot go long without drinking.

### How Much Water to Carry
A reasonable minimum is 3 litres per person per day for drinking and cooking. For a crew of two on a 14-day passage, that is 84 litres just for consumption — before washing, dishwashing, or any other use.

Carry more than you think you need. Fill every tank, and consider carrying additional jerry cans lashed on deck as a reserve.

### Reducing Consumption
- **Use salt water for washing dishes** and the initial rinse of hands and bodies. A foot-operated salt water pump at the galley sink is one of the best water-saving modifications you can make.
- **Cook with salt water** for boiling pasta and rice (mix 50/50 with fresh water, or use full salt water and skip adding salt).
- **Use a pressure sprayer** for freshwater rinses. A garden-style pump sprayer uses a fraction of the water compared to running a tap.

### If You Have a Watermaker
A watermaker removes the anxiety of water rationing entirely. Even a small DC unit producing 5 litres per hour gives you 20-30 litres per day — more than enough for two people to be comfortable. But always carry enough stored water to complete the passage without it. Watermakers break, and the passage is not the place to discover that yours needs a new membrane.

## Snacks and Watch Fuel

Do not underestimate the importance of snack food. Night watches are long, cold, and boring. Easy-to-eat snacks that the watch-keeper can grab without going below make a real difference to morale and alertness.

Stock up on:
- Trail mix and nuts
- Chocolate and energy bars
- Dried fruit
- Biscuits and crackers
- Hard sweets or ginger chews (also help with mild nausea)
- Thermos of hot soup or tea prepared at watch change

## The Provisioning Checklist

Before departure, run through this:
- [ ] Meals planned for passage duration plus 50% buffer
- [ ] 2-3 pre-cooked meals ready to reheat
- [ ] All water tanks full, plus reserve containers
- [ ] Snack bags pre-made for each watch
- [ ] Tinned goods marked and stowed low
- [ ] Fresh produce inspected, wrapped, and hung in nets
- [ ] Cooking fuel (gas bottles) checked — carry a spare
- [ ] Medication, including seasickness remedies for the first 48 hours
