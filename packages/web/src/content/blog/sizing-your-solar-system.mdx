## Start With Your Power Budget

Before buying a single panel, you need to know how much energy you actually use. Most sailors underestimate consumption by 30-50% because they forget about duty cycles, inverter losses, and the reality that fridges run 24 hours a day.

The honest way to do this: track every device on your boat, its wattage, and how many hours per day it runs. Our [Energy Planner](/tools/solar) automates this calculation, but understanding the principles matters.

## The Big Consumers

On a typical 40-foot cruising boat, these items dominate your power budget:

- **Refrigeration** — 45-65W running, but with a 33-50% duty cycle, that's 350-780 Wh/day. This is usually your single biggest consumer.
- **Autopilot** — 40-60W under sail, potentially 10-20 hours per day on passage. That's 400-1200 Wh/day when sailing.
- **Watermaker** — 100-300W for 2-3 hours per day. High peak draw but limited runtime.
- **Electronics** — Chartplotter, radar, AIS, instruments. Individually small, collectively significant: 200-400 Wh/day on passage.

A moderate cruiser at anchor typically uses 1500-2500 Wh/day. On passage, add autopilot and instruments: 2500-4000 Wh/day.

## Solar Panel Sizing

The formula is deceptively simple:

**Panel watts = Daily consumption / (Peak sun hours x derating factor)**

Peak sun hours vary by latitude and season. The Mediterranean averages 5-6 hours in summer, 2-3 in winter. The Caribbean is more consistent at 4-5 hours year-round.

The derating factor accounts for real-world losses: panel temperature, wiring, MPPT efficiency, partial shading. Use 0.75 as a conservative starting point.

For a 2000 Wh/day boat in the Med summer:
- 2000 / (5.5 x 0.75) = 485W of panels

Round up to 500W — two 250W panels or three 175W panels.

## Panel Types

**Rigid mono-crystalline** are the gold standard: highest efficiency per square metre, durable, 25-year warranties. Mount on a bimini frame or arch. Brands like Victron BlueSolar and Renogy deliver consistent quality.

**Semi-flexible** panels are tempting for curved surfaces. They work, but expect 10-15% less output than rigid equivalents and shorter lifespans (5-8 years vs 20+). Heat buildup on deck-mounted panels further reduces output.

**Flexible stick-on** panels are convenient but compromise: lower efficiency, heat problems, and delamination risk. Use them only where rigid panels genuinely cannot fit.

## Battery Bank Sizing

Your battery bank needs to store enough energy to get through low-production periods. The standard approach:

**Usable capacity = Daily consumption x Days of autonomy**

For LiFePO4 at 80% depth of discharge with 2 days of autonomy:
- 2000 Wh x 2 / 0.80 = 5000 Wh = 417 Ah at 12V

For AGM at 50% DoD:
- 2000 Wh x 2 / 0.50 = 8000 Wh = 667 Ah at 12V

This is why LiFePO4 has taken over the cruising world — you need 40% less capacity for the same usable energy, at a third of the weight.

## The MPPT Controller

An MPPT (Maximum Power Point Tracking) controller is non-negotiable for any serious solar installation. It converts the panel's higher voltage to your battery voltage while maximising power extraction.

Size your MPPT for your total panel wattage. A 500W array on a 12V system needs a controller rated for at least 42A (500W / 12V). The Victron SmartSolar range is the most popular choice in the cruising community for good reason: reliable, Bluetooth monitoring, and excellent documentation.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Not accounting for winter** — If you plan to cruise year-round, size for the worst season, not the best.
2. **Ignoring shading** — A shadow across one cell can cut panel output by 80%. Plan your mounting carefully.
3. **Undersized wiring** — Voltage drop in long cable runs wastes energy. Use our calculator or refer to ABYC standards.
4. **No monitoring** — Without a battery monitor (SmartShunt, BMV-712), you're guessing. Install one.
