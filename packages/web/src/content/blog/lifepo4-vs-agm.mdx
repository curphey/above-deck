## The Short Answer

If you're building a new system from scratch and can afford the upfront cost, go LiFePO4. If you're replacing existing AGM batteries on a budget and your charging system is simple, AGM still makes sense.

The longer answer depends on your boat, your budget, and how you sail.

## Capacity and Weight

This is where LiFePO4 wins decisively. At 80% usable depth of discharge versus AGM's 50%, you need significantly less rated capacity:

| Need | LiFePO4 | AGM |
|------|---------|-----|
| 200 Ah usable | 250 Ah (28 kg) | 400 Ah (120 kg) |
| 400 Ah usable | 500 Ah (56 kg) | 800 Ah (240 kg) |

On a 40-foot sailboat where every kilogram matters, saving 80-180 kg in the bilge is transformative. That weight was sitting at the lowest point of your boat, affecting motion comfort and performance.

## Cycle Life

A quality LiFePO4 cell delivers 3000-5000 cycles at 80% DoD. AGM batteries typically manage 400-800 cycles at 50% DoD.

In practice, a cruiser cycling batteries daily:
- **LiFePO4**: 8-14 years before significant degradation
- **AGM**: 1-2 years if deep-cycled daily, 3-4 years with careful management

## Charging Characteristics

LiFePO4 batteries accept charge at nearly full rate until almost completely full. AGM batteries taper their charge acceptance significantly above 80% state of charge, meaning the last 20% takes disproportionately long.

This matters enormously for solar charging. On a partly cloudy day with intermittent sun, LiFePO4 batteries capture far more of the available energy because they accept whatever current is available at any state of charge.

## The Real Cost

A 200Ah LiFePO4 bank costs $1800-2200 (Victron, Battle Born, Relion). A comparable 400Ah AGM bank costs $800-1200 (Lifeline, Trojan).

But factor in replacement cycles:
- Over 10 years: 1 set of LiFePO4 ($2000) vs 3-4 sets of AGM ($3200-4800)
- LiFePO4 is cheaper per usable amp-hour over its lifetime

The catch: LiFePO4 requires a compatible charging system. If your alternator regulator, shore charger, and solar controller can't be programmed for lithium charge profiles, you'll need to upgrade those too. Budget $500-1500 for charging system upgrades.

## When AGM Still Makes Sense

- **Starter batteries** — Always use AGM or lead-acid for engine starting. LiFePO4 can work but offers no advantage and adds BMS complexity.
- **Tight budget** — If $800 is your total battery budget, quality AGM (Lifeline GPL series) with careful charge management will serve you well.
- **Simple systems** — If you don't have solar and charge primarily from the alternator underway, AGM's simpler charging requirements are an advantage.
- **Cold climates** — LiFePO4 cannot be charged below 0C without a heated BMS. If you sail in near-freezing conditions, this matters.

## Practical Recommendations

**Going LiFePO4:** Buy from established marine brands (Victron, Battle Born, Relion, Lithionics). Ensure your BMS disconnects at low temperature and low voltage. Upgrade your alternator regulator to a lithium-compatible unit (Wakespeed WS500 or Balmar MC-614). Program your MPPT controller for the correct LiFePO4 charge profile.

**Staying with AGM:** Choose Lifeline GPL series — they're the standard in the cruising community for good reason. Never discharge below 50% SoC. Ensure your charging system reaches proper absorption voltage (14.2-14.4V for 12V systems). A battery monitor is essential to avoid accidental deep discharge.

## The Bottom Line

LiFePO4 is the better technology for cruising boats by almost every measure except initial cost. The weight savings alone justify the investment on most boats. But if budget constraints are real, a well-managed AGM system will still get you across oceans — sailors have been doing it for decades.
