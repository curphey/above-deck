## Why Engine Maintenance Matters

Your diesel engine is the most mechanically complex system on your boat, and it is also your backup plan when the sails cannot get you home. A well-maintained marine diesel will run for thousands of hours with remarkable reliability. A neglected one will leave you drifting.

The good news is that marine diesel maintenance is straightforward. Most tasks require basic tools, no special training, and less than an hour of your time. The key is consistency — doing the work on schedule rather than waiting for problems.

## Daily Pre-Start Checks

Before you turn the key each day, spend two minutes on these checks. They will catch problems before they become failures:

- **Engine oil level.** Pull the dipstick, wipe it, reinsert, and check. The level should be between the two marks. Top up if low, but also investigate why — consumption or leaks both need attention.
- **Coolant level.** Check the expansion tank or header tank. If it is low, you may have a leak in the cooling system. Never open a pressurised cap when the engine is hot.
- **Raw water strainer.** Look through the clear bowl. If it is full of weed, barnacles, or debris, clean it before starting. A blocked strainer means no cooling water, which means an overheated engine.
- **Belt tension.** Press the alternator belt with your thumb. It should deflect about 10-15mm. A loose belt will not charge your batteries properly and will wear faster. A belt that is too tight will damage alternator bearings.
- **Visual inspection.** Look for drips, leaks, loose hoses, or anything that was not there yesterday. Your engine compartment should be clean enough that new problems are immediately visible.

## Oil Changes

Oil changes are the single most important maintenance task. Marine diesels work hard — they run at higher loads than car engines and operate in a humid, salty environment.

**Frequency:** Every 100-150 engine hours, or at least once a year if you do not reach that many hours. Check your engine manual for the specific interval.

**Procedure:**
1. Run the engine for 5-10 minutes to warm the oil. Warm oil drains more completely and carries more contaminants out.
2. Close the raw water seacock.
3. Pump the oil out through the dipstick tube using a manual or electric extraction pump.
4. Replace the oil filter. Pre-fill the new filter with fresh oil if you can, and wipe a thin film of oil on the gasket.
5. Add the correct quantity of fresh oil. Use oil rated for marine diesel engines — typically 15W-40 meeting the engine manufacturer's specification.
6. Run the engine briefly, check for leaks around the filter, then shut down and recheck the level after a few minutes.

## Fuel Filters

Diesel fuel on boats is prone to contamination. Water ingress from condensation in tanks, biological growth (diesel bug), and sediment can all clog your fuel system and stop your engine at the worst possible time.

Most marine diesels have two fuel filters in series:

- **Primary filter/water separator** (like a Racor). This is your first line of defence. It has a clear bowl at the bottom where water collects. Drain it regularly and replace the element every 200-300 hours or annually.
- **Secondary filter** (on-engine). This is a finer filter that catches anything the primary missed. Replace it at every oil change.

**Carry spares.** Always have at least two sets of each filter on board, plus the correct O-rings. Being unable to replace a clogged fuel filter offshore is a situation you want to avoid entirely.

## Impeller Replacement

The raw water pump uses a flexible rubber impeller to pull seawater through your engine's heat exchanger. These impellers wear out — the vanes lose flexibility and eventually break off, sending rubber pieces into your cooling system.

**Frequency:** Replace annually, or every 300 hours, whichever comes first. Many cruisers replace at the start of each season.

**Procedure:**
1. Close the raw water seacock.
2. Remove the pump cover plate (usually 4-6 screws).
3. Pull the old impeller out with impeller removal pliers or two screwdrivers. Note the direction of rotation.
4. Inspect the old impeller. Count the vanes — if any are missing, you need to find the pieces. Check downstream in the heat exchanger.
5. Lubricate the new impeller with a thin coat of dish soap or glycerine (never petroleum-based lubricant).
6. Push the new impeller in, bending the vanes in the correct direction. Replace the cover plate with a new gasket.
7. Open the seacock and check for leaks.

## Winterization

If you are leaving your boat in a cold climate where temperatures drop below freezing, winterization protects your engine from cracked blocks, split hoses, and corroded internals.

The essentials:
- **Change the oil and filters.** Dirty oil contains acids that will corrode engine internals over the winter.
- **Fill the cooling system with antifreeze.** Close the raw water seacock, disconnect the raw water intake hose, and run the engine with the intake in a bucket of non-toxic antifreeze until it flows from the exhaust.
- **Fog the cylinders.** Spray fogging oil into the air intake while the engine runs, then shut down. This coats the cylinder walls and valves with a protective film.
- **Top up the fuel tank** and add a biocide to prevent diesel bug growth over the winter months.
- **Protect the exterior.** Spray exposed metal surfaces with a corrosion inhibitor. Cover the exhaust outlet to prevent moisture ingress.

## Keep a Log

Record every maintenance task with the date and engine hours. A simple notebook in the engine compartment works, or use a spreadsheet. When you sell the boat, a complete maintenance log significantly increases its value — and during your ownership, it ensures nothing gets missed.
