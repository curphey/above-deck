## What Is a Passage Plan

Passage planning is a structured process for thinking through a voyage before you leave the dock. It is a legal requirement under SOLAS for commercial vessels, but the principles apply equally to anyone taking a boat to sea.

The formal framework taught by the RYA and MCA is known as APEM: Appraise, Plan, Execute, Monitor. In practice, most recreational sailors do some version of this process informally. The value of the framework is that it makes the process systematic, so you are less likely to overlook something that matters.

The depth of each step scales with the passage. A weekend coastal sailor might spend 15 minutes on it. An offshore sailor might spend days. The framework is the same.

## Appraise

The first step is gathering information. Before you can plan a route, you need to understand what you are dealing with.

**Weather.** Obtain forecasts covering the full passage duration plus contingency time. For a coastal day sail, the morning forecast is sufficient. For an offshore passage, you want 5-7 days of GRIB data from at least two models (GFS and ECMWF), compared for consensus. Beyond 5 days, forecasts are useful for identifying general patterns but not for specific routing decisions.

**Tides.** Determine tidal heights at departure, arrival, and any constrained points along the route. Compute tidal stream rates and directions for each leg. Identify tidal gates, locations where the stream is so strong that passage is only practical during a limited window. Check where you are in the spring-neap cycle, as this affects both heights and stream rates by 30-40%.

**Charts and hazards.** Review the charts for your route and identify rocks, shoals, traffic separation schemes, restricted areas, and any seasonal hazards like fishing gear or wind farms. Check Notices to Mariners for recent changes. Note the charted depths at any constrained points and verify your under-keel clearance at the predicted tide height.

**Vessel and crew.** Assess your boat's condition, fuel state, and equipment readiness. Consider your crew's experience and physical state. A passage that is reasonable with a well-rested, experienced crew may be imprudent with a tired or inexperienced one.

## Plan

With information gathered, design the passage in detail.

**Route selection.** Plot a safe track with waypoints that maintain clearance from all hazards. Place waypoints outside harbour entrances, around headlands, and clear of traffic. Never place a waypoint directly on a navigation mark, as every other boat with a chart plotter will be converging on the same point.

**Departure time.** This is often the single most important planning decision for coastal passages. Your departure time determines whether you carry a fair tide or fight a foul one, whether you pass tidal gates at the right time, and whether you arrive at your destination with adequate depth. Work backwards from the constraints. If you need to pass a tidal gate at slack water and it takes four hours to reach it, your departure time is determined.

**Course to steer.** On any leg where tidal streams are significant, the direction you point the boat is not the same as the direction you want to go. The course to steer accounts for the vector sum of your boat speed through the water and the tidal stream. Ignoring this on a long coastal passage can set you miles off course.

**Contingency planning.** Identify bolt holes, ports of refuge you can divert to if conditions deteriorate or something breaks. Mark abort points on your route, positions where you consciously decide whether to continue or divert. Define weather thresholds that would trigger a plan change, such as wind exceeding a specific strength or visibility dropping below a certain distance.

**Fuel and water.** Calculate your range under power, accounting for sea state and current. Plan to arrive with at least 20% fuel remaining. For sailing passages, consider the likelihood of light winds requiring motoring and whether your fuel state allows for it.

## Execute

Put the plan into action.

**Pre-departure checks.** Engine, safety equipment, navigation lights, VHF radio, bilge pumps. These should be routine, but a checklist prevents complacency.

**Crew briefing.** Share the plan with everyone on board. Cover the route, expected weather, any tidal gates or timing-critical points, the location of bolt holes, and what conditions would trigger a diversion. Assign roles and discuss watch schedules for longer passages.

**Departure fix.** Confirm your position before leaving the harbour. This sounds obvious in the age of GPS, but verifying that your electronics agree with reality before you are offshore is basic seamanship.

**Navigation.** Follow the planned track, adjusting course to steer as tidal streams change through the passage. Monitor cross-track error. If the GPS shows you drifting off track, the tidal stream may be stronger than predicted, or the wind may be creating more leeway than expected.

## Monitor

A plan is a starting point, not a contract. Monitoring means continuously checking whether reality matches the plan and adjusting when it does not.

**Position fixing.** Regular position fixes using GPS, and where possible, cross-checked with visual bearings, radar, or depth soundings. GPS is reliable, but it is a single point of failure. Knowing your depth is consistent with the charted depth at your GPS position is a simple and effective cross-check.

**Weather monitoring.** Compare actual conditions against the forecast. Is the wind doing what the model predicted? Is the barometric pressure following the expected trend? If conditions are deteriorating faster than forecast, consider diverting early rather than waiting until your options narrow.

**ETA checks.** Compare your actual progress against planned timing. Are you going to arrive at the tidal gate on time? If you are falling behind, do you need to increase speed, adjust your route, or revise your plan entirely?

**Decision points.** At each abort point you identified during planning, make a conscious decision: continue or divert. This is easier said than done. There is a strong psychological tendency to continue with a plan once committed. Having pre-defined abort criteria helps counter this.

## The Data You Need

A practical summary of the data sources behind each element of a passage plan:

- **Weather:** GRIB files from GFS, ECMWF, or ICON; text forecasts from your national weather service; synoptic pressure charts for the big picture.
- **Tides:** Predicted heights from tide tables or a tidal API (NOAA for US waters); tidal stream data from charts, stream atlases, or current predictions.
- **Charts:** Up-to-date electronic charts covering your route and approaches, including any ports of refuge.
- **Port information:** Approach details, depths, facilities, VHF channels, and customs requirements at your destination and bolt holes.
- **Vessel data:** Your polar diagram (for sailing performance), fuel consumption rates, and range under power.
