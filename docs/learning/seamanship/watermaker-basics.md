## Why Carry a Watermaker

Fresh water is the single biggest constraint on how long you can stay away from a marina. A typical cruising couple uses 20-40 litres per day for drinking, cooking, and basic washing. With a 300-litre tank, that gives you 7-15 days before you need to find a source.

A watermaker changes this equation entirely. With the ability to make 40-80 litres per hour from seawater, you can stay at anchor indefinitely, take proper freshwater showers, and stop worrying about rationing. For cruisers who spend extended time in remote anchorages, it is arguably the most life-improving upgrade you can make.

## How Reverse Osmosis Works

All marine watermakers use reverse osmosis (RO). The principle is straightforward:

1. **Seawater intake.** A pump draws raw seawater through a strainer and pre-filter, removing sediment, sand, and biological material.
2. **High-pressure pump.** This is the heart of the system. It pressurises the filtered seawater to 55-70 bar (800-1000 PSI). This pressure is what forces water molecules through the membrane while rejecting salt and contaminants.
3. **RO membrane.** A semi-permeable membrane allows pure water molecules to pass while blocking 95-99% of dissolved salts, bacteria, and other contaminants. The rejected brine is discharged overboard.
4. **Product water.** The fresh water passes through a post-filter (usually activated carbon) to improve taste, then flows into your freshwater tank.

The water quality is excellent — typically 300-500 parts per million (PPM) total dissolved solids, well within drinking water standards. For comparison, typical tap water is 200-500 PPM.

## Power Consumption

Power is the main consideration when choosing a watermaker. There are three broad categories:

### AC-Powered Units (Mains/Generator)
Traditional watermakers like the Spectra Newport or Village Marine draw 1-2 kW and require AC power — either shore power, a generator, or a large inverter. If you already run a generator daily, these are efficient and proven. If you are trying to be generator-free, they are impractical.

### DC-Powered Units
Newer designs run directly on 12V or 24V DC. The Spectra Ventura and Rainman DC units are popular examples. They typically draw 8-15 amps at 12V (100-180 watts) and produce 5-8 litres per hour. The lower output means longer run times, but the power consumption is manageable with a good solar array. A 400W solar setup can comfortably run one of these during peak sun hours.

### Manual/Low-Power Units
The Katadyn PowerSurvivor is a hand-operated RO unit that produces about 1 litre per hour with no electrical power. It is a survival tool rather than a cruising convenience, but worth mentioning as a backup.

## Choosing the Right Size

Match the watermaker to your actual needs and electrical budget:

- **Solo or couple, moderate use:** A DC unit producing 5-8 litres per hour, run for 4-6 hours daily, gives you 20-48 litres — enough for comfortable cruising.
- **Family or higher consumption:** An AC unit producing 40-80 litres per hour, run for 1-2 hours with the generator, covers a family of four easily.
- **Power budget matters most.** Do not buy a unit you cannot run. A watermaker that sits idle because you cannot spare the power is wasted money and space.

## Installation Considerations

A few things to get right during installation:

- **Seawater intake location.** The intake must be well below the waterline, away from any through-hulls that discharge grey water, sewage, or engine cooling water. Contaminated intake water will damage the membrane and produce unsafe product water.
- **Pre-filtration.** A good pre-filter setup (typically a 20-micron sediment filter followed by a 5-micron filter) protects the membrane and extends its life significantly. Carry plenty of spare filter cartridges.
- **Product water diverter.** The first few minutes of product water after startup will be higher in salinity. Install a three-way valve or automatic diverter that sends initial product water overboard until salinity drops to an acceptable level.
- **TDS meter.** A total dissolved solids meter on the product water line tells you the membrane is performing correctly. If PPM climbs above 500, investigate.

## Maintenance and Preservation

The RO membrane is the most expensive component in the system, and it is also the most vulnerable. Two things will destroy it: biological growth and chlorine.

### Regular Use
If you run the watermaker every day or two, the flow of water keeps the membrane clean. This is the ideal scenario — daily use is the best maintenance.

### Short-Term Storage (Up to a Week)
If you are not going to use the watermaker for a few days, flush it with fresh water. Most units have an automatic or manual freshwater flush cycle. This displaces the salt water and reduces the chance of biological growth.

### Long-Term Storage (More Than a Week)
For longer periods, you need to preserve the membrane by circulating a biocide solution (typically sodium metabisulphite mixed with fresh water) through the system. This prevents bacteria and algae from colonising the membrane surface.

**The procedure:**
1. Flush the system with fresh water.
2. Mix the biocide solution according to the manufacturer's instructions.
3. Circulate the solution through the system using the low-pressure pump.
4. Close all valves to keep the solution in contact with the membrane.
5. Repeat the preservation process every 1-3 months if the boat is sitting idle.

Neglecting to preserve a membrane that sits unused is the most common and most expensive mistake cruisers make with watermakers. A new membrane costs hundreds of dollars and takes time to source — often unavailable in remote cruising grounds.

## Practical Tips

- **Run it in clean water.** Avoid making water in harbours, near river mouths with sediment, or in areas with visible pollution. Anchor in clean, open water for your water-making sessions.
- **Monitor the output.** Check product water salinity regularly. A gradual increase in PPM indicates the membrane is aging or needs cleaning. A sudden spike means something is wrong.
- **Keep spares on board.** Pre-filters, O-rings, and a rebuild kit for the high-pressure pump are essential spares. The membrane itself is worth carrying if you are heading to remote areas.
