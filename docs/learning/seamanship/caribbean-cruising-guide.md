## Why the Eastern Caribbean

The Eastern Caribbean island chain is one of the most popular cruising grounds in the world, and for good reason. Consistent trade winds of 15-25 knots, warm water, short hops between islands, and a mix of cultures and landscapes make it ideal for both first-time and experienced cruisers.

The arc of islands stretching from the Virgin Islands in the north down to Grenada and Trinidad in the south offers hundreds of anchorages, each with its own character. You can spend a single season or many years exploring without running out of new places to visit.

## The Typical Route

Most cruisers arriving from across the Atlantic make landfall in Barbados or one of the Windward Islands (Martinique, St. Lucia, or Grenada). From there, the classic route works either north or south along the island chain.

### Northbound (Leeward Route)
Starting from Grenada or St. Lucia, you sail north through the Grenadines, St. Vincent, Martinique, Dominica, Guadeloupe, Antigua, and up to the Virgin Islands. The prevailing trade winds blow from the east and northeast, so northbound passages along the leeward (western) side of the islands are generally comfortable — you are sailing in the wind shadow with relatively flat water.

### Southbound
Coming from the Virgin Islands, the southbound route means sailing to windward. Passages tend to be rougher, with the trade winds on the nose or close-hauled. Many cruisers time their southbound transit in late spring or early summer when the trades are lighter.

### Island Hopping Distances
Most inter-island hops are between 20 and 60 nautical miles — comfortable day sails. A few longer passages (like Guadeloupe to Antigua at roughly 40 NM, or Martinique to Dominica at about 25 NM) are still manageable in daylight. The short distances mean you can set your own pace and wait for weather windows without losing days.

## Weather and Seasons

### Trade Wind Season (December-May)
This is prime cruising season. The trade winds blow steadily from the east-northeast at 15-25 knots. Skies are mostly clear with occasional rain squalls. Seas are moderate. This is when the majority of cruisers are active in the islands, and it is the most comfortable time to sail.

### Transition Months (June and November)
The trades become less reliable, with lighter and more variable winds. Occasional tropical waves move through, bringing unsettled weather for a day or two. Many cruisers use June to position south toward Grenada or Trinidad before hurricane season fully sets in.

### Hurricane Season (June 1 - November 30)
The official season runs six months, but the peak risk is August through October. Hurricanes and tropical storms can affect any part of the Caribbean, though the southern islands (Grenada, Trinidad, Bonaire, Curacao) are statistically less likely to receive direct hits.

**Where to be during hurricane season:**
- **Trinidad and Tobago** — south of the usual hurricane belt, with good haul-out yards and a large cruising community that stays year-round.
- **Grenada** — increasingly popular, with several yards offering hurricane storage. Slightly more risk than Trinidad but convenient.
- **ABC Islands (Aruba, Bonaire, Curacao)** — south of the hurricane belt and rarely affected.
- **On the hard** — if staying in the hurricane zone, the safest option is hauled out with the boat properly secured. Never ride out a hurricane at anchor or on a mooring.

## Popular Anchorages and Stops

### The Grenadines
The island chain between St. Vincent and Grenada is the highlight of the Eastern Caribbean for many cruisers. Tobago Cays (a marine park with turquoise water and reef protection), Bequia (the "island of the clouds" with a lively harbour town), and Mayreau are standouts. Anchorages are well-protected and the scenery is exceptional.

### Martinique
A French overseas department with excellent provisioning — boulangeries, fromageries, and well-stocked supermarkets. The town of Le Marin in the south has one of the largest anchorages in the Caribbean and is a major services hub for cruisers (sailmakers, mechanics, chandleries).

### Dominica
Wild, mountainous, and relatively undeveloped. The island is known for its lush rainforest, hot springs, and welcoming people. Portsmouth in the north (Prince Rupert Bay) is the main cruiser anchorage. Local boat boys will offer to take your lines and guide you to moorings — the PAYS (Portsmouth Association of Yacht Services) system is well-organised and worth supporting.

### Antigua
English Harbour and Falmouth Harbour are steeped in naval history. Nelson's Dockyard is a UNESCO World Heritage site and one of the most atmospheric marinas in the Caribbean. Antigua Race Week in late April draws the sailing community from across the region.

### British Virgin Islands
The BVI is where many people learn to charter and sail in the Caribbean. The Bight at Norman Island, the caves at Treasure Point, the Baths at Virgin Gorda, and the pizza at Willy T's are all part of the experience. Anchorages are well-charted and close together. It is busier and more commercial than the southern islands, but deservedly popular.

## Practical Considerations

### Customs and Immigration
Each island nation has its own entry requirements. You must clear in and out of each country — this means visiting customs and immigration with your boat papers, passports, and crew list. Some islands offer online pre-clearance (like the BVI and Antigua). Carry printed copies of your boat documentation and several copies of your crew list.

### Provisioning
Provisioning quality varies significantly. French islands (Martinique, Guadeloupe, St. Martin) have excellent supermarkets with European products. Smaller islands in the Grenadines have limited shops with basic supplies at higher prices. Stock up in larger islands before heading to remote ones.

### Anchoring Etiquette and Mooring Fields
Many popular anchorages now have mooring fields to protect the seabed. Use mooring balls where provided and pay the fees — they support marine conservation. Where anchoring is permitted, set your hook properly, use adequate scope, and check for good holding. Sand is generally excellent holding; grass patches are poor.

### Budget
The Caribbean is not cheap, especially in tourist areas and the BVI. Marina fees, mooring fees, customs charges, and provisioning costs add up. Anchoring out and cooking on board keeps costs down significantly. Dominica, the Grenadines (outside of Mustique), and Grenada tend to be more affordable than the Virgin Islands or St. Barts.

## Getting There

Most boats reach the Eastern Caribbean via the Atlantic Rally for Cruisers (ARC) from the Canary Islands, or independently from Europe, West Africa, or the US East Coast. The passage from the Canaries to the Caribbean is roughly 2,700 nautical miles and typically takes 14-21 days with the trade winds.

From the US East Coast, the Intracoastal Waterway to Florida followed by a passage through the Bahamas to the Caribbean is a common route. Allow time for weather windows in the Gulf Stream and Bahamas.
