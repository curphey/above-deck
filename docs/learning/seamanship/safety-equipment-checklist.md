## Safety Gear Is Not Optional

The ocean does not care about your experience level or the size of your boat. Proper safety equipment is the difference between an incident and a catastrophe. Every item on this list exists because someone, somewhere, needed it and either had it or did not.

This checklist covers the essentials for offshore cruising — passages of 50+ nautical miles from shore. Coastal sailing has different requirements, but if you are equipping for offshore, you are covered for everything else.

## Emergency Communication

### EPIRB (Emergency Position Indicating Radio Beacon)
An EPIRB is your last resort — it sends a distress signal via satellite to search and rescue authorities worldwide, along with your GPS position. Modern EPIRBs (Category I) are float-free: if the boat sinks, they activate automatically.

- Register your EPIRB with your national authority. An unregistered EPIRB delays rescue response.
- Check the battery expiry date annually. Replace before it expires.
- Test monthly using the built-in self-test function.
- Mount it in a location that is accessible but also where the hydrostatic release can work if the boat goes down.

### VHF Radio with DSC
Your primary communication tool. A fixed-mount VHF with Digital Selective Calling (DSC) can send an automated distress alert with your position at the press of a button. Ensure it is connected to your GPS for this to work.

Carry a handheld VHF as backup, waterproof, with fresh batteries. If you abandon to a life raft, the handheld goes with you.

### Personal Locator Beacon (PLB)
A PLB is like a personal EPIRB — small enough to clip to your lifejacket or harness. If you go overboard and the crew cannot find you, a PLB sends your position to rescue services. At around 200-300 dollars, it is one of the best investments in personal safety you can make.

## Man Overboard Equipment

A crew member going overboard offshore is one of the most dangerous situations on a sailing boat. Recovery is difficult, especially in rough seas or at night. Preparation and equipment save lives.

### Essentials
- **Lifesling or throwing line.** A Lifesling is a floating horseshoe attached to a long polypropylene line. Sail a circle around the person in the water, and the line sweeps to them. Practice the procedure before you need it.
- **Man overboard pole with flag.** A tall, weighted pole with a bright flag and drogue that you throw immediately when someone goes over. The flag stays visible even in waves when a person's head does not.
- **Strobe light and whistle.** Attached to the MOB pole or a dedicated MOB marker. At night, the strobe is essential for keeping visual contact.
- **Swim ladder or boarding system.** Getting a person back on board from the water is physically challenging, especially if they are injured, exhausted, or hypothermic. A dedicated boarding ladder that deploys from the stern, or a system of blocks and a halyard for lifting, is essential. Practice this in calm conditions.

### Crew Equipment
Every person on watch offshore should wear:
- An inflatable lifejacket with integrated harness, crotch strap, and a spray hood
- A tether clipped to jacklines or strong points at all times on deck
- A PLB or AIS beacon clipped to the lifejacket

## Life Raft

If the boat is sinking and you have exhausted all options to save it, the life raft is your next home. The old saying is worth repeating: "Step up into the life raft" — meaning, do not get in until the boat is genuinely going down.

- **Size it for your crew** plus at least one extra person.
- **Inspect and service** on the schedule recommended by the manufacturer — typically every 3 years, sometimes annually for older rafts.
- **Mount it where you can deploy it quickly** — in a deck cradle or a transom mount, not buried in a locker under a pile of gear.
- **Include a grab bag** stored next to the life raft with: water, food, first aid kit, handheld VHF, PLB, flares, knife, signalling mirror, and medications.

## Fire Safety

Fire on a boat is terrifying because you cannot leave. Prevention and fast response are critical.

### Fire Extinguishers
- Carry a minimum of three: one in the galley, one near the engine compartment, and one in the main cabin.
- Use dry chemical (ABC-rated) for general use. A CO2 extinguisher near the engine compartment is effective for electrical and fuel fires.
- Check pressure gauges monthly. Service or replace according to manufacturer guidelines.
- **Know how to use them.** Pull the pin, aim at the base of the fire, squeeze the handle, sweep side to side. Practice with an expired extinguisher so the motion is automatic.

### Engine Compartment
An automatic fire suppression system in the engine compartment is highly recommended. These activate when the temperature reaches a threshold, suppressing the fire even if no one is in the compartment.

### Fire Blanket
Keep a fire blanket within reach of the galley. For stovetop fires, a blanket is often more effective and less messy than an extinguisher.

## Flares and Visual Signals

Despite modern electronics, visual distress signals remain required and useful.

- **Parachute flares** (SOLAS-rated): Carry at least 4. These fire a bright red flare to 300 metres altitude, visible for 25+ nautical miles at night.
- **Handheld red flares:** Carry at least 4. Used for close-range signalling when rescuers are in the area.
- **Orange smoke signals:** Carry at least 2. Effective during daylight for helicopter or ship rescue.
- **Check expiry dates.** Expired flares are unreliable and may not satisfy regulatory requirements, but carry them anyway as backups.

## First Aid

- Carry a comprehensive offshore first aid kit, not a basic household one. Include supplies for wound closure (sutures or staples), splinting, burns, and medications for infection, pain, and seasickness.
- Take a marine first aid course. Knowing how to stabilise a fracture, treat hypothermia, or manage a serious wound when you are days from help is knowledge that matters.
- Include any prescription medications your crew needs, plus extras in case of delay.

## Inspect and Practice

Equipment that you have never tested is equipment you cannot rely on. At the start of each season:

- Deploy your Lifesling and practice a man overboard drill.
- Check all flare expiry dates and battery levels on EPIRBs, PLBs, and handheld VHF.
- Verify that your life raft service is current.
- Test fire extinguisher pressure gauges.
- Review emergency procedures with your crew: who does what, where the gear is, how to make a mayday call.

Safety gear is an investment you hope never to use, but when you need it, nothing else matters.
