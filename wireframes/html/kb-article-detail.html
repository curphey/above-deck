Sizing Your Solar System

How to calculate the right amount of solar panel wattage and battery capacity for your boat, based on what you actually use and where you sail.

b:victron s:solar e:mppt t:peak-sun-hours t:energy-audit t:pvgis

Start With an Energy Audit

Before you can size a solar system, you need to know what you're actually powering. Work through every circuit on your boat and list the wattage of each device alongside a realistic daily run time in hours. Multiply those two numbers to get watt-hours per day per item, then sum the column. Most coastal cruisers land somewhere between 80 and 150 Ah per day at 12 V; bluewater boats with a watermaker, an autopilot running continuously, and a fridge plus a freezer can push past 250 Ah without much effort.

Measured data is worth far more than estimates. If your boat has a Victron BMV or a Cerbo GX already installed, pull two weeks of history and look at the average daily discharge. That number is your baseline. If you're fitting out a new vessel, use a clamp meter to capture running current on each load. The fifteen minutes you spend on an energy audit will save you from either under-speccing a system that leaves your batteries flat every afternoon or over-speccing one that costs three times what the job needed.

Understanding Peak Sun Hours

Solar panel ratings are measured at Standard Test Conditions — 1,000 W/m² of irradiance and a cell temperature of 25°C — which rarely match real-world conditions on a pitching deck. What matters for sizing is peak sun hours (PSH): the equivalent number of hours per day at 1,000 W/m² that matches the total daily solar irradiance at your location and time of year. A Mediterranean anchorage in July might deliver 6.5 PSH; a Scottish sea loch in November might scrape 1.5. The European Commission's PVGIS tool lets you enter any latitude and longitude and extract monthly average irradiance data — use it, and size for your worst month, not your best.

Cruising Area Peak Sun Hours 600 W Panel Daily Output
Mediterranean (summer) 6.0 – 7.0 300 – 350 Wh
Caribbean 5.0 – 6.0 250 – 300 Wh
UK / Northern Europe 3.0 – 4.5 150 – 225 Wh
Pacific (tropics) 5.5 – 6.5 275 – 325 Wh

The Sizing Formula

Required panels (W) = Daily consumption (Wh) ÷ Peak sun hours × 1.3 (derating factor)

The 1.3 derating factor accounts for real-world losses: MPPT controller efficiency (~97%), wiring resistance, dust and partial shading on the panels, and elevated cell temperatures reducing output by roughly 0.4% per degree above 25°C. For a worked example, a boat consuming 120 Ah/day at 12 V (1,440 Wh) sailing in the Caribbean at 5.5 PSH needs a minimum of 1,440 ÷ 5.5 × 1.3 = 340 W of panel. In practice, fitting 400 W gives comfortable headroom and covers overcast days without needing to run the engine.

Battery Bank Sizing

Your panels charge your batteries, so the battery bank has to be large enough to absorb peak generation and supply power overnight. A common rule is to size the usable bank at two to three days of consumption. LiFePO4 chemistry allows 80–90% depth of discharge repeatably over 2,000+ cycles, making it the preferred choice for liveaboards despite the higher upfront cost. AGM batteries are limited to around 50% DoD to preserve cycle life, so an equivalent AGM bank must be nearly twice the nominal capacity of a lithium one. Either way, a solar MPPT controller needs to be matched to your panel open-circuit voltage and your battery bank voltage — check the controller's maximum input voltage against your panel string configuration before buying.

A good rule of thumb: your usable battery capacity should cover at least two days of consumption without any charging.
